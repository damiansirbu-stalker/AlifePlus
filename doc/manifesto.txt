Manifesto

AlifePlus continues what GSC Game World started and never finished.
The original A-Life was an event-driven simulation where NPCs lived autonomous lives, found items, traded, responded to threats, and pursued goals independently of the player.
Most of it was cut before Shadow of Chernobyl shipped, and what remained was smart terrains and campfires.

Every design choice in AlifePlus traces back to the original engine architecture or to documented GSC developer intent.
Nothing is invented and nothing is faked.

--

I. Architecture

1. Event-driven contract

The fundamental architectural decision in AlifePlus is that nothing runs unless the engine says something happened.
AlifePlus subscribes to engine callbacks and evaluates when they fire, rather than scanning the game world or iterating entities on a timer -- if nothing happens in the simulation, the mod consumes zero cycles.

The engine defines 54 callback types in GameObject::ECallbackType (game_object_space.h), and a single death event crosses five layers: CEntityAlive::Die (entity_alive.cpp:321) fires the eDeath Lua callback, GE_DIE propagates as a net event (xrServer_process_event.cpp:184), game_sv_Single::on_death (game_sv_single.cpp:338) notifies the simulator, alife().on_death (alife_simulator_base2.cpp:84) processes the A-Life death, and notify_on_member_death (alife_online_offline_group.cpp:305) updates the squad.
Smart terrain transitions propagate through on_location_change (alife_monster_detail_path_manager.cpp:275).

The legacy movement code in alife_monster_abstract.cpp contains a commented-out call to alife().check_for_interaction(this), marked with #pragma todo("Do not forget to uncomment here!!!").
This was the hook point for NPC-NPC encounters during A-Life graph traversal -- entities meeting each other as they moved between levels, but the interaction logic was never connected.
AlifePlus implements encounter evaluation through cause predicates that fire on the same graph transitions the engine was supposed to use.

"A character's life now worked in such a way, that he took the quest generated by some smart terrain, then walked to the destination it had set for him. After getting there, he would get points, and the smart terrain would take him under control for some time."
-- Dmitriy Iassenev, Game Developer (2008) [1]

-

2. Radiant evaluation pattern

Squads evaluate their surroundings when they transition between smart terrains, which is how A-Life naturally works -- a squad arrives at a new location and looks around for stashes, unguarded outposts, or threats.

on_location_change (alife_monster_detail_path_manager.cpp:275) fires on every graph node transition, and Anomaly's sim_board.script hooks this as squad_on_enter_smart and squad_on_leave_smart.
CALifeMonsterBrain::select_task (alife_monster_brain.cpp:118) iterates all registered smart terrains and picks the highest-scoring one, time-gated by smart_terrain_choose_interval from INI and guarded by can_choose_alife_tasks (exposed to Lua).

AlifePlus adds a predicate evaluation layer at the transition point, registering each radiant cause for both enter and leave callbacks to get two evaluation moments per transition.
The predicate checks nearby world state within a configurable perception radius -- stashes, unguarded smarts, elite presence -- and publishes a typed cause event when the predicate matches.
The triggering squad is both the sensor and the actor, so there is one evaluation per one actor and no god loop searching for responders.
This is the same pattern you would use in any event-sourced system: the entity that observes the condition is the entity that acts on it.

"I was very happy with the work done when I followed a stalker from one level to another, saw how he searched for artifacts, found them, returned to the dealer, approached him, traded, picked a new quest, went on -- too bad this did not make it into the original game."
-- Dmitriy Iassenev, Game Developer (2008) [1]

-

3. Physical simulation guarantee

No entities are spawned out of thin air, no items are teleported, and no squads are created to fill a role -- every creature, item, or object involved in any AlifePlus consequence was already there living its own A-Life.
This is an invariant, not a guideline.

The engine moves entities through a three-layer stack: CALifeMonsterBrain selects a smart terrain target (alife_monster_brain.cpp:118), CALifeMonsterMovementManager resolves the path type, and CALifeMonsterDetailPathManager executes A* graph traversal (alife_monster_detail_path_manager.cpp:124), computing distance from speed, time delta, and time factor at each vertex in follow_path (line 219).
When the target vertex belongs to a different level, the entity has physically traveled there through alife_graph_registry::change (alife_graph_registry_inline.h:17), which atomically updates the entity's graph ID, position, and node.
Squads sync all member positions to the commander (alife_online_offline_group.cpp:55-79), and online/offline switching is distance-based with hysteresis (alife_switch_manager.cpp).

Squads walk to their destinations, and if they die on the way they die -- the engine handles combat if they encounter enemies, and the consequence resolves only if they arrive.
I chose this constraint deliberately because it is the only way to get emergent gameplay that the player can trust: if a squad shows up at your stash, it walked there from somewhere real, through real danger, and you could have intercepted it on the way.

"The player is not the most powerful or the strongest -- he is one of many, whose lives crossed the Zone."
-- Alexey Sytianov, Shpil Magazine [2]

-

4. Event pipeline

AlifePlus was built on reverse-engineered documentation of 162 engine and script callbacks, catalogued with fire rates, payload structures, and engine-side guards from real gameplay sessions in TestZone (a companion component that instruments the engine at runtime).

The pipeline is a classical pub/sub chain: engine callbacks (squad_on_npc_death, squad_on_enter_smart, squad_on_leave_smart) and synthetic hooks feed into a producer that gates events through a budget distributor, on-map resolver, Bresenham ratio gate, class-isolated cooldowns, per-cause rate limit, and a predicate.
Typed cause events publish to xbus, and the consumer dispatches consequences in priority order, stopping the chain on OK_STOP or ERROR_STOP.
Causes and consequences are fully decoupled -- causes publish, consequences subscribe, and neither references the other, which means adding a new consequence to an existing cause is a one-file change with zero coupling to the cause code.

-

5. Dual-rate event partitioning

30 maps simulate simultaneously, and without any bias over 97% of cause events would fire on maps the player never sees -- which makes the mod useless for the person actually playing.

The engine already had the answer: setup_current_speed (alife_monster_detail_path_manager.cpp:211) checks whether the entity is on the player's level and selects m_fCurrentLevelGoingSpeed or m_fGoingSpeed accordingly (alife_movement_manager_holder.h:15-16).
Both speeds are exposed to Lua as travel_speed() and current_level_travel_speed() (xrServer_Objects_ALife_Monsters_script4.cpp:56).
GSC designed the engine to simulate the player's level faster than background levels, which is exactly what you want -- things happen near the player at full speed, and the rest of the world ticks along at a lower rate.
Anomaly set both values to 3 (m_stalker.ltx:372-373), which neutralized this split entirely and made all 30 maps equivalent.

AlifePlus restores this differential at the cause evaluation layer using a Bresenham ratio gate that partitions event slots between the player's map and everything else.
On-map and off-map have separate class-isolated cooldowns so one cannot starve the other, and at the default setting of 8 the ratio is 4:1 favoring on-map.
The slider goes from -10 (off-map only) to +10 (on-map only).
This is not a new mechanic -- it is restoring a design decision that GSC made in the engine and Anomaly accidentally removed.

-

6. Synthetic callback mechanism

Where the engine lacks a callback for something AlifePlus needs to observe, a runtime hook emits a synthetic event without modifying base scripts: AddScriptCallback declares a new callback name, SendScriptCallback dispatches to all registered handlers, and the hook wraps an existing function at runtime via _G module table replacement, calling the original before emitting the new event.
AddScriptCallback and SendScriptCallback are defined in Anomaly's dynamic_callbacks.lua, and Anomaly's own engine extensions use the same mechanism -- pseudogiant stomp callbacks (CPseudoGigant__OnStartStompAnimation, CPseudoGigant__BeforeHitCallback), net spawn/destroy lifecycle hooks, and the bullet callback chain all go through it.
AlifePlus uses the same pattern for the same reason: extending the event contract without touching base code.

-

7. Cascade prevention

The guard chain prevents cascades through multiple layers: a global cooldown gates all causes, per-cause sliding windows limit individual fire rates, per-consequence cooldowns prevent flooding, and area-based deduplication stops massacre chains from spiraling by suppressing any massacre that fires in an area where one already fired within the decay window.
This is a standard rate-limiting stack -- the same thing you would build for any event-driven system where downstream handlers can trigger upstream conditions.

-

8. Symmetric entity treatment

Causes fire for all creatures equally and consequences resolve for all factions equally -- the simulation does not revolve around the player and does not give the player special treatment.

AlifePlus filters out traders, quest givers, companions, mechanics, barmen, leaders, guides, and story characters before any squad selection, protecting over 80 named characters through a shared is_unscriptable predicate in xlibs.
Quest targets and dependants are not protected, because A-Life can and should affect their fate -- your target gets in a fight with another stalker and dies before you reach him, and you fail the quest.
This is how STALKER has worked since Shadow of Chernobyl, and I kept it that way on purpose because protecting quest targets from the simulation would mean faking it.

You are not special.

-

9. Shared abstraction layer

All engine work is extracted into xlibs, which provides single-call squad search with filter predicates instead of manual SIMBOARD iteration, safe server object resolution with nil guards, stash discovery with radius and distance sorting, a chase lifecycle manager that handles scripted movement with arrival polling and re-scripting, PDA message dispatch, level and creature type resolution, and a pub/sub event bus.
I built xlibs for my own mods, but the goal is that any modder can call find_squads with a filter or chase to start a pursuit, without needing to know which engine objects are server-side, which params are squad IDs vs NPC IDs, or how SIMBOARD iteration works.
STALKER modding shouldn't require you to crash 10 times before discovering some undocumented engine quirk.

II. Mechanics

STASH: loot, ambush, fill

A squad transitions at a smart terrain, discovers a nearby stash, and walks there to loot it, camp to ambush others, or hide supplies inside depending on faction.
The NPC physically walks the distance and can be intercepted, killed, or beaten to the stash.

The engine provides CInventoryBox as a dumb container and eInvBoxItemTake as an actor-only callback (game_object_space.h:54).
Anomaly's treasure_manager.script indexes all inventory boxes on load, fills half with random loot, and tracks them for the player, but NPCs never touch stashes in vanilla Anomaly.
All NPC involvement with stashes is narrative: surrender dialogs reveal locations (dialogs.script), task rewards mark them on the map (xr_effects.script), and PDA messages hint at contents (ui_pda_npc_tab.script).
The npc_on_item_take_from_box callback exists in axr_main.script but its handler is empty -- the infrastructure was built but nobody connected it.
Stashes sit across the Zone ignored by every NPC, waiting exclusively for the player, and that is the simplified version that shipped, not the intended design.

AlifePlus connects the infrastructure that was left hanging: squads discover stashes through radiant evaluation (#2), walk there through physical simulation (#3), and interact with the contents on arrival.

"The character would take on a quest to search for the artifact, go to the approximate area in which it could be found, if able to find the artifact, brought it back to the dealer, traded it, then picked a new quest."
-- Dmitriy Iassenev, Game Developer (2008) [1]

Iassenev described this exact loop working internally at GSC and regretted it never shipped.

--

HARVEST: hunt

When an NPC or the player picks up an artefact, nearby outlaws see an opportunity and pursue the carrier through the chase API -- real movement, real risk, and the carrier can fight back or outrun them.

The engine fires eOnItemTake through CInventoryOwner::OnItemTake (InventoryOwner.cpp:334) for both actor and NPCs, so the callback infrastructure covers everyone.
CSE_ALifeAnomalousZone had a spawn_artefacts method that is commented out in the engine source (xrServer_Objects_ALife_Monsters_script.cpp:118), and Anomaly moved artefact spawning to scripts (bind_anomaly_zone.script) while adding NPC artefact gathering via xr_gather_items.script with community filters and timers.
The artefact economy was clearly planned at the engine level and partially implemented in Anomaly, but nobody added the competitive response -- when someone picks up something valuable, others should care.

The harvest cause subscribes to both actor_on_item_take and npc_on_item_take, checks IsArtefact() on the picked item, and publishes to xbus.
The consequence finds nearby hostile squads and starts a chase where the carrier becomes a moving target.

"In early versions of the simulator, a character knew one or several dealers, which had sets of quests that they generated based on the map of anomalous activity and requests from organizations, which wanted to get rich from artifacts found in the Zone."
-- Dmitriy Iassenev, Game Developer (2008) [1]

Artefacts had value in Iassenev's original vision, NPCs competed for them, and dealers drove the economy.
AlifePlus restores the competitive response that makes artefacts worth fighting over.

--

ELITE: promote, buffs

An NPC accumulates kills and levels up through 10 tiers, each granting better ammo, grenades, and engine rank boosts.

The engine defines CHARACTER_RANK_VALUE (character_info_defs.h:23) with the Russian comment "ot 0 (sovsem neopytnyi) do >100 (ochen' opytnyi)" -- from 0 (completely inexperienced) to over 100 (very experienced).
Killers gain rank points scaled by the victim's bracket through rank_kill_points (relation_registry_actions.cpp:290), ranging from 5 for a novice kill to 20 for a legend kill.
ai_stalker.cpp:749 computes rank-dependent combat properties with the comment "vychislit' immuneta v zavisimosti ot ranga" -- compute immunities depending on rank.
Rank clamps to 0-100 and interpolates between novice and experienced coefficients for dispersion, immunity, and visibility, but in Anomaly only dispersion actually varies (1.0 to 0.8, giving a 20% accuracy improvement at max rank) while immunity and visibility are both set to 1.0/1.0, making them dead code.

So the engine already has NPC progression from kills, GSC wrote Russian comments explaining how it should work, and then Anomaly flattened two out of three combat properties to no-ops.
AlifePlus uses the same kill tracking to derive elite tiers and applies the buffs that the rank system was designed for but never got: AP ammo selection based on the weapon's best k_ap, grenade restocking scaled by tier, and rank threshold boosts that push NPCs into higher engine brackets where the dispersion actually matters.

"Every character in the game always has a goal: to uncover the mystery of the Zone."
-- Dmitriy Iassenev, Game Developer (2008) [1]

--

ELITEKILL: bounty, targeted

When an elite dies, the killer receives rubles scaled by the elite's level and other elites on the same level pursue the killer across the map -- killing a dangerous NPC attracts dangerous attention.

The death publishes to xbus like any other kill event through the event pipeline (#4).
The bounty consequence rewards the killer directly, and the targeted consequence queries the tracker for other elites on the same level, excludes the victim and killer, and starts a chase sequence for each hunter using the same chase lifecycle that drives revenge and harvest pursuits.
One chase implementation, multiple causes -- this is the kind of reuse you get from a properly decoupled pub/sub architecture.

"One of the goals that developers set for themselves when creating the mechanics of the Faction War is for the player to see what his actions lead to."
-- Clear Sky pre-release interview [5]

GSC wanted actions to have visible consequences in the world, and elitekill is the purest expression of that -- you killed someone important, and now you deal with the fallout.

--

MASSACRE, SQUADKILL, BASEKILL: scavenge, investigate, revenge, flee, support

When deaths pile up, factions respond -- scavengers converge on massacre sites, victims' factions investigate, survivors flee to the nearest base or rally to defend it, and squads pursue killers across the map.

notify_on_member_death (alife_online_offline_group.cpp:305) fires when a squad member dies, and Anomaly's sim_board propagates this as squad_on_npc_death with three server objects: the squad, the dead member, and the killer.
All three causes are reactive and subscribe to the same death callback but evaluate different patterns: massacre checks accumulated deaths at a smart terrain against a threshold within a decay window, squadkill checks whether the last squad member is dying, and basekill checks whether the smart is a faction base with deaths exceeding the base threshold.
Each publishes a separate typed cause and consequences subscribe independently -- scavenge looks for scavenger factions, investigate looks for the victim's faction, and revenge starts a chase to the killer.

This is the part of AlifePlus that generates the most emergent stories: a massacre at the Army Warehouses draws bandits to scavenge, which draws Duty to investigate, which leads to another fight, and the player walks into the aftermath without knowing any of it was scripted by the mod.
The cascade prevention layer (#7) keeps this from spiraling into an infinite loop.

"The in-game Zone has been shaken by furious war of factions for the new territories, resources, and control points of the important paths to the center of the Zone."
-- Clear Sky official gameplay page [4]

"To notify player about the offline activity, we used news, which were generated if some character could see an event or its consequences."
-- Dmitriy Iassenev, Game Developer (2008) [1]

Iassenev's chain was: events generate consequences, consequences generate news.
AlifePlus implements exactly that: events fire causes, causes dispatch consequences, and consequences generate PDA messages.

--

WOUNDED: hunt, help

When an NPC or the player uses a healing item, nearby predator mutants sense weakness and move toward the wounded while nearby same-faction squads rush to help.

The engine already has a complete monster behavior state machine in state_defs.h covering approach (eStateEat_CorpseApproachRun), inspection (eStateEat_CheckCorpse), dragging to cover (eStateEat_Drag), eating (eStateEat_Eat), and resting (eStateEat_Rest).
CMonsterCorpseMemory (monster_corpse_memory.cpp) scans visible entities for dead CEntityAlive objects, tracks them by distance, and returns the nearest via find_best_corpse.
Corpses have a food value (CEntityAlive::m_fFood = 100 * mass) that depletes as monsters eat, and squad members lock corpses to prevent competition (monster_state_eat_inline.h:54).
The chimera has a dedicated hunting/ambush state (chimera_state_hunting_inline.h), and GetSatiety() returns a hardcoded 0.5 while ChangeSatiety() does nothing (base_monster.h:169-173) -- the hunger system was planned and partially coded but never activated, though the state machine survived intact.

No npc_on_item_use callback exists anywhere in Anomaly, and xr_eat_medkit.script handles NPC medkit usage through consume_medkit which calls alife_release_id and npc:change_health directly without firing any event -- the NPC heals, and nobody knows it happened.
AlifePlus hooks consume_medkit at runtime via _G module table replacement, calls the original, and emits x_npc_medkit_use through AddScriptCallback/SendScriptCallback without modifying base scripts.
This is the synthetic callback mechanism (#6) in action.

"I would very much like to play a game in which characters would live their own lives, each would have his own goal in the game, each would have human-like (or some specific for monsters) needs that the character would have to satisfy."
-- Dmitriy Iassenev, Game Developer (2008) [1]

"A complex AI system with a life simulation system that enables any monster and NPC to act independently on any game level, with creatures having a full life cycle where they hunt, feed, take rest, and sleep."
-- Oles Shishkovtsov, ModDB interview [3]

Shishkovtsov described exactly what the engine code contains: eStateRest with Sleep handles rest and sleep, eStateEat with the full corpse lifecycle handles feeding, eStateAttack handles hunting, and eStatePredator with ambush behavior handles predator tactics.
The state machine is there, the hunger system was half-built, and the behavior tree covers the full lifecycle Shishkovtsov described.
AlifePlus extends the hunt trigger from "corpse detected" to "wounded NPC detected" -- if monsters were designed to converge on corpses, they should converge on the wounded too.

--

AREA: conquer

When a squad discovers an unguarded smart terrain nearby, it walks there and claims the area if it arrives alive.

The engine's CSE_ALifeSmartZone (xrServer_Objects_ALife.h:373) is a bare scheduling shell with zero faction fields -- all territory control is Lua-side.
Anomaly's smart_terrain.script derives self.faction from which stalkers are physically present (check_smart_faction, line 1209), and sim_board.script gates squad-to-smart assignment by faction and time of day -- enemy bases can only be raided between 3:00 and 6:00, enemy territory between 9:00 and 15:00 (general_base_precondition, general_territory_precondition).
The faction system exists and the time-gating exists, but squads never proactively decide to claim territory -- they only go where the sim_board tells them to go.

AlifePlus extends this with radiant conquest through runtime mutation of the smart terrain's respawn parameters: when a squad conquers a smart, the arrival handler sets faction_controlled and respawn_params at runtime so the engine's try_respawn spawns the conqueror's faction.
The same radiant evaluation pattern (#2) drives it -- a squad at a smart terrain sees a nearby unguarded position, walks there through the physical simulation (#3), and the arrival callback applies the mutation.
Conquered smarts are tracked with FIFO eviction and persist across save/load through a two-phase restore that re-applies mutations after the engine rebuilds smart terrain data from INI.
Territory changes hands through movement, not through scripts.

"A control point leading to a place with artifacts is controlled by bandits. Their destruction means that the path to resources is safe. The player will see how the stalkers of his group go to the place with artifacts."
-- Clear Sky pre-release interview [5]

"The more points with artifacts the faction controls, the cooler stalkers it can recruit."
-- Clear Sky pre-release interview [5]

Clear Sky's faction war was supposed to be exactly this -- factions fighting over control points that affect resource access and recruitment quality.
AlifePlus implements the mechanic at the A-Life level rather than through scripted mission triggers.
