# Manifesto

AlifePlus is loyal to GSC's original A-Life vision and the X-Ray engine architecture it was built on. The original A-Life was an event-driven simulation where NPCs lived autonomous lives -- found items, traded, responded to threats, and pursued goals independently of the player. Most of it was cut before Shadow of Chernobyl shipped. What remained was smart terrains and campfires.

Every design choice traces back to the original engine architecture or to documented GSC developer intent. Nothing is invented and nothing is faked.

---

## Principles

### Event-Driven Contract

Nothing runs unless the engine says something happened. Callbacks fire, predicates evaluate, consequences dispatch. If nothing happens in the simulation, the mod consumes zero cycles.

The engine defines 54 callback types in `game_object_space.h`. A single death event crosses five layers: entity death fires the Lua callback, propagates as a net event, notifies the simulator, processes the A-Life death, and updates the squad. Smart terrain transitions propagate through `on_location_change` in the detail path manager.

The legacy movement code in `alife_monster_abstract.cpp` contains a commented-out call to `alife().check_for_interaction(this)`, marked with `#pragma todo("Do not forget to uncomment here!!!")`. This was the hook point for NPC-NPC encounters during A-Life graph traversal -- entities meeting each other as they moved between levels. The interaction logic was never connected. AlifePlus fires cause predicates on the same graph transitions.

> "A character's life now worked in such a way, that he took the quest generated by some smart terrain, then walked to the destination it had set for him. After getting there, he would get points, and the smart terrain would take him under control for some time."
> -- Dmitriy Iassenev, Game Developer (2008) [1]

### Physical Simulation Guarantee

Nothing is spawned, teleported, or created to fill a role. Every creature, item, or object involved in any consequence was already there, living its own A-Life. This is an invariant, not a guideline.

The engine moves entities through a three-layer stack: the brain selects a smart terrain target, the movement manager resolves the path type, and the detail path manager executes A* graph traversal, computing distance from speed, time delta, and time factor at each vertex. When the target vertex belongs to a different level, the entity has physically traveled there through the graph registry, which atomically updates position and node. Online/offline switching is distance-based with hysteresis.

Squads walk to their destinations, and if they die on the way they die. The consequence resolves only if they arrive. If a squad shows up at your stash, it walked there from somewhere real, through real danger, and you could have intercepted it on the way.

> "The player is not the most powerful or the strongest -- he is one of many, whose lives crossed the Zone."
> -- Alexey Sytianov, Shpil Magazine [2]

### Symmetric Entity Treatment

Causes fire for all creatures equally and consequences resolve for all factions equally. The simulation does not revolve around the player.

Traders, quest givers, companions, mechanics, barmen, leaders, guides, and story characters are filtered out before any squad selection -- over 80 named characters protected through a shared predicate in xlibs. Quest targets are not protected, because A-Life can and should affect their fate. Your target gets in a fight and dies before you reach him, and you fail the quest. This is how STALKER has worked since Shadow of Chernobyl. Protecting quest targets from the simulation would mean faking it.

You are not special.

## Architecture

### Event Pipeline

Classical pub/sub chain: engine callbacks and synthetic hooks feed into a producer that gates events through a budget distributor, on-map resolver, Bresenham ratio gate, class-isolated cooldowns, per-cause rate limit, and a predicate. Typed cause events publish to `xbus`, and the consumer dispatches consequences in priority order, stopping the chain on `OK_STOP` or `ERROR_STOP`.

Causes and consequences are fully decoupled -- causes publish, consequences subscribe, neither references the other.

### Radiant Evaluation

Squads evaluate their surroundings when they transition between smart terrains. A squad arrives at a new location and looks around for stashes, unguarded outposts, threats, or unmet needs.

`on_location_change` fires on every graph node transition. Anomaly hooks this as `squad_on_enter_smart` and `squad_on_leave_smart`. The engine's task selection iterates all registered smart terrains and picks the highest-scoring one, time-gated by configuration and guarded by `can_choose_alife_tasks`.

A predicate evaluation layer sits at the transition point, checking nearby world state within a configurable perception radius and publishing a typed cause event when the predicate matches. This is where all radiant causes originate -- stash, area, and needs. The triggering squad is both sensor and actor: one evaluation per one actor, no iteration over the game world.

> "I was very happy with the work done when I followed a stalker from one level to another, saw how he searched for artifacts, found them, returned to the dealer, approached him, traded, picked a new quest, went on -- too bad this did not make it into the original game."
> -- Dmitriy Iassenev, Game Developer (2008) [1]

### Synthetic Callbacks

Where the engine lacks a callback, a runtime hook emits a synthetic event without modifying base scripts. `AddScriptCallback` declares a new callback name, `SendScriptCallback` dispatches to all registered handlers, and the hook wraps an existing function at runtime via module table replacement, calling the original before emitting the new event.

Anomaly's own engine extensions use the same mechanism -- pseudogiant stomp callbacks, net spawn/destroy lifecycle hooks, and the bullet callback chain all go through `AddScriptCallback`/`SendScriptCallback`.

### A-Life Ratio

30 maps simulate simultaneously. The same causes fire and the same consequences resolve on every map. The rules are identical. The frequency is not.

The engine carries two speeds on every entity: `m_fGoingSpeed` for background levels and `m_fCurrentLevelGoingSpeed` for the current level. Two update paths run in parallel: current-level entities update every render frame with a real-time budget, while all offline entities share a round-robin pool. Inside the current level: full physics, animations, combat. Outside: statistical. With ~30 maps, background events outnumber current-level events roughly 50:1.

Anomaly configured both speed values to the same number, removing the movement speed differential that GSC built into the engine.

A-Life Ratio is a Bresenham ratio gate that restores this differential at the cause evaluation layer, partitioning event slots between the current level and background levels. Both sides have separate class-isolated cooldowns so one cannot starve the other. The default yields a 4:1 admission ratio compensating for the ~50:1 imbalance. The slider goes from -10 (background only) to +10 (current level only).

## Mechanics

Every mechanic below: observe an engine event, evaluate a predicate, dispatch a consequence using real entities already in the simulation.

### Massacre: Scavenge, Investigate

When deaths accumulate at a location, nearby scavenger factions converge on the site and the victims' faction sends squads to investigate.

`notify_on_member_death` fires on squad member death. `monster_corpse_memory` already makes individual monsters converge on corpses -- scanning for dead entities, tracking by distance, returning the nearest through `find_best_corpse`, approaching, inspecting, dragging, and eating through a full state chain in `state_defs.h` with corpse locking to prevent competition. Scavenge scales this convergence from individual corpses to massacre sites. `relation_registry` tracks kill-based attitude shifts between factions but nothing acts on the change -- investigate acts on it.

> "To notify player about the offline activity, we used news, which were generated if some character could see an event or its consequences."
> -- Dmitriy Iassenev, Game Developer (2008) [1]

### Squad Kill: Revenge, Flee

When the last member of a squad dies, same-faction squads pursue the killer. Nearby squads of the victim's faction fall back to the nearest base.

`relation_registry_actions` adjusts faction goodwill on kills, scaled by the victim's rank -- the engine tracks who killed whom and shifts attitudes, but nothing acts on the change. Revenge pursues the killer through real movement across real distance. The engine's stalker AI has danger evaluation states that trigger retreat -- flee routes squads to the nearest friendly base through `sim_board`.

> "One of the goals that developers set for themselves when creating the mechanics of the Faction War is for the player to see what his actions lead to."
> -- Clear Sky pre-release interview [5]

### Base Kill: Support, Flee

When a faction base sustains enough deaths, nearby friendly squads converge to reinforce and squads at the base evacuate.

Clear Sky's faction war routed reinforcements to contested control points. `sim_board` gates squad-to-base assignment through faction preconditions -- support uses the same routing when the base death threshold is breached.

> "The in-game Zone has been shaken by furious war of factions for the new territories, resources, and control points of the important paths to the center of the Zone."
> -- Clear Sky official gameplay page [4]

### Elite: Promote

An NPC accumulates kills and levels up through 10 tiers, gaining AP ammo, grenades, and engine rank boosts.

`CHARACTER_RANK_VALUE` in the engine has the Russian comment "from 0 (completely inexperienced) to over 100 (very experienced)." `rank_kill_points` awards points scaled by the victim's bracket. The engine computes rank-dependent combat properties ("compute immunities depending on rank"), interpolating between novice and experienced coefficients for dispersion, immunity, and visibility. Anomaly sets immunity and visibility to 1.0/1.0 -- dead code. Only dispersion varies, giving 20% accuracy improvement at max rank.

- **AP ammo.** The engine defines `k_ap` (armor penetration) per weapon. An experienced fighter using basic rounds after dozens of kills is the fake version.
- **Grenades.** NPC grenade usage is built into the combat AI, but NPCs exhaust their initial supply permanently. Without restocking, no NPC ever throws a grenade past early game.
- **Rank boost.** Pushes NPCs into engine brackets where `CHARACTER_RANK_VALUE` dispersion curves actually apply.

### Elite Kill: Bounty, Targeted

When an elite dies, the killer receives rubles scaled by the elite's level and other elites on the same tier pursue the killer.

`rank_kill_points` already rewards kills with rank -- bounty extends this with rubles. The engine tracks kill-based progression but has no consequence for killing high-rank entities. Targeted adds that consequence: kill someone dangerous, attract dangerous attention.

### Wounded: Hunt, Help

When an NPC or the player uses a healing item, nearby predator mutants close in and nearby same-faction squads rush to help.

The engine has a complete predator behavior tree: `eStatePredator` with ambush states, `chimera_state_hunting` with dedicated pursuit, `eStateAttack` for engagement. `monster_corpse_memory` drives convergence on dead entities. `GetSatiety()` returns a hardcoded 0.5 while `ChangeSatiety()` does nothing -- the hunger-driven hunt cycle was planned and abandoned, but the predator state machine survived intact.

No `npc_on_item_use` callback exists in Anomaly. `xr_eat_medkit.script` heals the NPC directly without firing any event. A synthetic callback hooks the handler at runtime and emits `x_npc_medkit_use` without modifying base scripts.

The predator state machine targets dead entities. Wounded entities are weakened prey -- the same behavioral trigger with a living target.

> "A complex AI system with a life simulation system that enables any monster and NPC to act independently on any game level, with creatures having a full life cycle where they hunt, feed, take rest, and sleep."
> -- Oles Shishkovtsov, ModDB interview [3]

### Harvest: Hunt

When an NPC or the player picks up an artefact, nearby outlaws pursue the carrier through real movement.

The engine fires item take callbacks for both actor and NPCs. Anomaly's `xr_gather_items.script` implements NPC artefact gathering with community filters and timers. The artefact economy was planned at the engine level -- artefact spawning methods exist in the engine source as commented-out code. The competitive response was never added.

> "In early versions of the simulator, a character knew one or several dealers, which had sets of quests that they generated based on the map of anomalous activity and requests from organizations, which wanted to get rich from artifacts found in the Zone."
> -- Dmitriy Iassenev, Game Developer (2008) [1]

### Stash: Loot, Ambush, Fill

A squad discovers a nearby stash on smart terrain transition, walks there to loot it, camp to ambush others, or hide supplies inside. The NPC physically walks the distance and can be intercepted or beaten to the stash.

The engine provides inventory boxes as containers. Anomaly's `treasure_manager` indexes all inventory boxes on load, fills half with random loot, and tracks them for the player -- but NPCs never interact with stashes. All NPC involvement is narrative: surrender dialogs reveal locations, task rewards mark them on the map, PDA messages hint at contents. `npc_on_item_take_from_box` exists but its handler is empty. The infrastructure was built and left disconnected.

> "The character would take on a quest to search for the artifact, go to the approximate area in which it could be found, if able to find the artifact, brought it back to the dealer, traded it, then picked a new quest."
> -- Dmitriy Iassenev, Game Developer (2008) [1]

### Area: Conquer

When a squad discovers an unguarded smart terrain nearby, it walks there and claims it on arrival.

The engine's smart zone is a bare scheduling shell with zero faction fields -- all territory control is Lua-side. Anomaly derives faction from which stalkers are physically present, and `sim_board` gates squad-to-smart assignment by faction and time of day. Squads never proactively decide to claim territory.

On arrival, the handler sets faction and respawn parameters at runtime so the engine spawns the conqueror's faction. Conquered smarts persist across save/load with FIFO eviction.

> "A control point leading to a place with artifacts is controlled by bandits. Their destruction means that the path to resources is safe."
> -- Clear Sky pre-release interview [5]

> "The more points with artifacts the faction controls, the cooler stalkers it can recruit."
> -- Clear Sky pre-release interview [5]

### Needs: 9 Drives, 15 Consequences

When a squad transitions at a smart terrain, it evaluates nine human needs and pursues whichever is strongest.

Drive scoring uses Hull's Drive Reduction Theory (1943) weighted by Maslow's hierarchy of needs (1943). `drive = weight * (elapsed / threshold)^2`. Weights follow Maslow -- survival outweighs security outweighs belonging. The squared deprivation ratio adapts Hull: drive grows nonlinearly with deprivation, so mild discomfort is noise and critical need dominates.

Hull and Maslow were the dominant behavioral models when GSC designed A-Life in 2002-2004. The engine's need states (`eStateEat`, `eStateRest`, Sleep) map directly to Maslow's physiological tier. `GetSatiety()` is a continuous deprivation variable -- the same concept Hull formalized as drive strength.

The nine drives:

- **Hunger** -- finds a campfire, eats from inventory (bread, sausage, canned goods)
- **Sleep** -- waits for nightfall, finds a campfire, sleeps through the night
- **Rest** -- finds a campfire, smokes a cigarette, has a drink
- **Heal** -- returns to a friendly base, uses a medkit, bandage, or stimpack
- **Shelter** -- returns to a friendly base when exposed too long
- **Money** -- searches anomaly fields for artefacts, or hunts mutant lairs
- **Supply** -- visits a trader, exchanges an artefact for ammo, grenades, or medical supplies
- **Job** -- guards their base, explores the Zone, researches anomalies. Monolith and Greh worship. Military and Duty drill
- **Social** -- finds a campfire or returns to base, shares cigarettes and drinks

NPCs consume inventory items on arrival -- food, cigarettes, drinks, medkits, or artefacts depending on the need.

Engine scaffolding:

- `GetSatiety()` returns hardcoded 0.5, `ChangeSatiety()` does nothing -- hunger planned and abandoned
- `eStateRest` with Sleep, `eStateEat` with full corpse lifecycle -- states exist, nothing triggers them by need
- `sr_camp.script` runs campfire social dynamics
- `gulag_general.script` assigns campfire, rest, and guard jobs
- `xr_eat_medkit.script` handles NPC healing

> "It was planned that character behaviour would diversify due to introduction of requirements of food and sleep."
> -- Dmitriy Iassenev, Game Developer (2008) [1]

> "Now, instead of creation of an algorithm for choice of current needs and their satisfaction, a simpler model has been used, which offloaded this work to the smart terrains."
> -- Dmitriy Iassenev, Game Developer (2008) [1]

> "I would very much like to play a game in which characters would live their own lives, each would have his own goal in the game, each would have human-like (or some specific for monsters) needs that the character would have to satisfy."
> -- Dmitriy Iassenev, Game Developer (2008) [1]

**Item consumption.** `GetSatiety()` returning 0.5 proves hunger was planned. `eStateEat` proves eating was planned. Walking to a campfire hungry and eating nothing is fake.

**Supply exchange.** Iassenev described the artefact economy: find artefact, bring to dealer, trade, pick new quest. Free items from a trader is a spawn, not a trade. The supply consequence requires an artefact.
