# Manifesto

AlifePlus is loyal to GSC's original A-Life vision and the X-Ray engine architecture it was built on. The original A-Life was an event-driven simulation where NPCs lived autonomous lives -- found items, traded, responded to threats, and pursued goals independently of the player. Most of it was cut before Shadow of Chernobyl shipped. What remained was smart terrains and campfires.

Every design choice in AlifePlus traces back to the original engine architecture or to documented GSC developer intent. Nothing is invented and nothing is faked.

---

## Principles

### Event-Driven Contract

The fundamental architectural decision is that nothing runs unless the engine says something happened. AlifePlus subscribes to engine callbacks and evaluates when they fire, rather than scanning the game world or iterating entities on a timer. If nothing happens in the simulation, the mod consumes zero cycles.

The engine defines 54 callback types in `game_object_space.h`. A single death event crosses five layers: the entity death fires the Lua callback, propagates as a net event, notifies the simulator, processes the A-Life death, and updates the squad. Smart terrain transitions propagate through `on_location_change` in the detail path manager.

The legacy movement code in `alife_monster_abstract.cpp` contains a commented-out call to `alife().check_for_interaction(this)`, marked with `#pragma todo("Do not forget to uncomment here!!!")`. This was the hook point for NPC-NPC encounters during A-Life graph traversal -- entities meeting each other as they moved between levels. The interaction logic was never connected. AlifePlus implements encounter evaluation through cause predicates that fire on the same graph transitions.

> "A character's life now worked in such a way, that he took the quest generated by some smart terrain, then walked to the destination it had set for him. After getting there, he would get points, and the smart terrain would take him under control for some time."
> -- Dmitriy Iassenev, Game Developer (2008) [1]

### Physical Simulation Guarantee

No entities are spawned out of thin air, no items are teleported, and no squads are created to fill a role. Every creature, item, or object involved in any AlifePlus consequence was already there, living its own A-Life. This is an invariant, not a guideline.

The engine moves entities through a three-layer stack: the brain selects a smart terrain target, the movement manager resolves the path type, and the detail path manager executes A* graph traversal, computing distance from speed, time delta, and time factor at each vertex. When the target vertex belongs to a different level, the entity has physically traveled there through the graph registry, which atomically updates position and node. Online/offline switching is distance-based with hysteresis.

Squads walk to their destinations, and if they die on the way they die. The engine handles combat if they encounter enemies, and the consequence resolves only if they arrive. If a squad shows up at your stash, it walked there from somewhere real, through real danger, and you could have intercepted it on the way.

> "The player is not the most powerful or the strongest -- he is one of many, whose lives crossed the Zone."
> -- Alexey Sytianov, Shpil Magazine [2]

### Symmetric Entity Treatment

Causes fire for all creatures equally and consequences resolve for all factions equally. The simulation does not revolve around the player and does not give the player special treatment.

AlifePlus filters out traders, quest givers, companions, mechanics, barmen, leaders, guides, and story characters before any squad selection, protecting over 80 named characters through a shared predicate in xlibs. Quest targets and dependants are not protected, because A-Life can and should affect their fate -- your target gets in a fight and dies before you reach him, and you fail the quest. This is how STALKER has worked since Shadow of Chernobyl. Protecting quest targets from the simulation would mean faking it.

You are not special.

## Architecture

### Event Pipeline

The pipeline is a classical pub/sub chain: engine callbacks and synthetic hooks feed into a producer that gates events through a budget distributor, on-map resolver, Bresenham ratio gate, class-isolated cooldowns, per-cause rate limit, and a predicate. Typed cause events publish to `xbus`, and the consumer dispatches consequences in priority order, stopping the chain on `OK_STOP` or `ERROR_STOP`.

Causes and consequences are fully decoupled -- causes publish, consequences subscribe, and neither references the other. Adding a new consequence to an existing cause is a one-file change with zero coupling to the cause code.

### Radiant Evaluation

Squads evaluate their surroundings when they transition between smart terrains, which is how A-Life naturally works -- a squad arrives at a new location and looks around for stashes, unguarded outposts, or threats.

`on_location_change` fires on every graph node transition. Anomaly hooks this as `squad_on_enter_smart` and `squad_on_leave_smart`. The engine's task selection iterates all registered smart terrains and picks the highest-scoring one, time-gated by configuration and guarded by `can_choose_alife_tasks`.

AlifePlus adds a predicate evaluation layer at the transition point. The predicate checks nearby world state within a configurable perception radius -- stashes, unguarded smarts, elite presence, unmet needs -- and publishes a typed cause event when the predicate matches. This is where radiant causes (stash, area, needs) originate: the squad looks around, evaluates what it sees and what it needs, and acts. The triggering squad is both the sensor and the actor: one evaluation per one actor, no god loop searching for responders.

> "I was very happy with the work done when I followed a stalker from one level to another, saw how he searched for artifacts, found them, returned to the dealer, approached him, traded, picked a new quest, went on -- too bad this did not make it into the original game."
> -- Dmitriy Iassenev, Game Developer (2008) [1]

### Synthetic Callbacks

Where the engine lacks a callback, a runtime hook emits a synthetic event without modifying base scripts. `AddScriptCallback` declares a new callback name, `SendScriptCallback` dispatches to all registered handlers, and the hook wraps an existing function at runtime via module table replacement, calling the original before emitting the new event.

Anomaly's own engine extensions use the same mechanism -- pseudogiant stomp callbacks, net spawn/destroy lifecycle hooks, and the bullet callback chain all go through `AddScriptCallback`/`SendScriptCallback`. AlifePlus uses the same pattern for the same reason: extending the event contract without touching base code.

### A-Life Ratio

30 maps simulate simultaneously. The same causes fire and the same consequences resolve on every map. The rules are identical. The frequency is not.

The engine carries two speeds on every entity: `m_fGoingSpeed` for background levels and `m_fCurrentLevelGoingSpeed` for the current level. Two update paths run in parallel: current-level entities update every render frame with a real-time budget, while all offline entities share a round-robin pool. Inside the current level: full physics, animations, combat. Outside: statistical. With ~30 maps, background events outnumber current-level events roughly 50:1.

Anomaly configured both speed values to the same number, removing the movement speed differential that GSC built into the engine.

AlifePlus restores this differential at the cause evaluation layer. A-Life Ratio is a Bresenham ratio gate that partitions event slots between the current level and background levels. Both sides have separate class-isolated cooldowns so one cannot starve the other. The default setting yields a 4:1 admission ratio that compensates for the ~50:1 imbalance. The slider goes from -10 (background only) to +10 (current level only). Nothing changes about what happens -- only the frequency at which it happens here versus elsewhere.

## Mechanics

Every mechanic below follows the same contract: observe an engine event, evaluate a predicate against world state, dispatch a consequence using real entities already in the simulation. Every mechanic traces to GSC's documented intent or engine source. Nothing is invented.

### Massacre: Scavenge, Investigate

When deaths pile up at a location, the area becomes a massacre site. Nearby scavenger factions converge to pick through the aftermath. The victims' faction sends squads to investigate.

The engine fires `notify_on_member_death` when a squad member dies. Anomaly propagates this as `squad_on_npc_death` with the squad, the dead member, and the killer. The massacre cause checks accumulated deaths at a smart terrain against a threshold within a decay window.

The engine already has convergence-on-death behavior at the creature level: `monster_corpse_memory` scans for dead entities, tracks them by distance, and returns the nearest via `find_best_corpse`. Monsters approach, inspect, drag, and eat corpses through a full state chain in `state_defs.h`. Squad members lock corpses to prevent competition. If individual monsters converge on a single corpse, factions should converge on a massacre site -- scavenge scales the same pattern from one body to many.

Investigation is the faction-level response to the same event. The engine's relation system in `relation_registry` tracks kill-based attitude changes between factions. When members die, faction goodwill shifts. The relation exists in engine memory but nobody acts on it. Investigate sends the victim's faction to the site where their people died.

> "To notify player about the offline activity, we used news, which were generated if some character could see an event or its consequences."
> -- Dmitriy Iassenev, Game Developer (2008) [1]

Iassenev's chain was: events generate consequences, consequences generate news. AlifePlus implements exactly that.

### Squad Kill: Revenge, Flee

When the last member of a squad dies, same-faction squads pursue the killer across the map. Nearby squads of the victim's faction fall back to the nearest base.

The squad kill cause subscribes to the same death callback as massacre but evaluates a different pattern: whether the last squad member is dying.

The engine's relation system already tracks kill-based retaliation at the data level. `relation_registry_actions` adjusts faction goodwill when kills occur -- the killer's faction loses standing with the victim's faction, scaled by the victim's rank. The engine remembers who killed whom and adjusts attitudes accordingly. But nothing acts on the changed attitude. Revenge activates what the relation system records: the victim's comrades pursue the killer through the chase API, using real movement across real distance, and the killer can fight back or escape.

Flee is the survival response. The engine's stalker AI has danger evaluation states that trigger retreat behavior when threats are detected. Flee routes surviving squads to the nearest friendly base through the same sim_board routing the engine uses for normal squad assignment. The squad physically walks to safety -- it can be intercepted on the way.

> "One of the goals that developers set for themselves when creating the mechanics of the Faction War is for the player to see what his actions lead to."
> -- Clear Sky pre-release interview [5]

### Base Kill: Support, Flee

When a faction base comes under sustained attack, nearby friendly squads rush to reinforce. Squads at the base evacuate to the nearest friendly position.

The base kill cause checks whether the smart terrain is a faction base with deaths exceeding the base threshold.

Clear Sky's faction war had reinforcement mechanics -- when a control point was attacked, the owning faction could send squads to defend it. The engine's sim_board already routes squads to faction bases through precondition gates. Support uses the same routing to converge nearby friendly squads when the base death threshold is breached. Flee evacuates remaining defenders to the nearest friendly position, the same survival response as squad kill flee.

> "The in-game Zone has been shaken by furious war of factions for the new territories, resources, and control points of the important paths to the center of the Zone."
> -- Clear Sky official gameplay page [4]

### Elite: Promote

An NPC accumulates kills and levels up through 10 tiers. Each tier grants AP ammo matched to the weapon's best penetration coefficient, grenades scaled by level, and engine rank boosts that improve combat accuracy.

The engine defines `CHARACTER_RANK_VALUE` with the Russian comment "from 0 (completely inexperienced) to over 100 (very experienced)." Killers gain rank points scaled by the victim's bracket through `rank_kill_points`. The engine computes rank-dependent combat properties with the comment "compute immunities depending on rank." Rank interpolates between novice and experienced coefficients for dispersion, immunity, and visibility -- but Anomaly only lets dispersion vary (20% accuracy improvement at max rank) while immunity and visibility are both set to 1.0/1.0, making them dead code.

The rank system was designed to make experienced NPCs meaningfully better fighters. AlifePlus activates what the engine already tracks:

- **AP ammo.** The engine defines `k_ap` (armor penetration) per weapon. An NPC that survived enough fights to reach elite status should use effective ammunition for his weapon class. The alternative -- an experienced fighter still carrying basic rounds after dozens of kills -- is the fake version.
- **Grenades.** NPC grenade usage is built into the engine's combat AI. Elites get restocked at tier thresholds because a veteran fighter should have grenades. Without restocking, NPCs exhaust their initial supply and never throw another grenade for the rest of the playthrough.
- **Rank boost.** Pushes the NPC into higher engine brackets where dispersion actually decreases and the combat properties that GSC wrote Russian comments for finally matter.

### Elite Kill: Bounty, Targeted

When an elite dies, the killer receives rubles scaled by the elite's level. Other elites on the same tier hunt the killer across the map. Killing a dangerous NPC attracts dangerous attention.

The bounty consequence rewards the killer directly. The targeted consequence queries for other elites at the same level and starts a chase for each hunter. One chase implementation, multiple causes -- the reuse you get from decoupled pub/sub.

> "One of the goals that developers set for themselves when creating the mechanics of the Faction War is for the player to see what his actions lead to."
> -- Clear Sky pre-release interview [5]

You killed someone important. Now you deal with the fallout.

### Wounded: Hunt, Help

When an NPC or the player uses a healing item, nearby predator mutants sense weakness and close in. Nearby same-faction squads rush to help the wounded.

The engine has a complete monster behavior state machine covering approach, inspection, dragging to cover, eating, and resting. Corpse memory scans visible entities for dead creatures, tracks by distance, and returns the nearest. The chimera has a dedicated hunting/ambush state. `GetSatiety()` returns a hardcoded 0.5 while `ChangeSatiety()` does nothing -- the hunger system was planned, partially coded, and never activated, though the state machine survived intact.

No npc_on_item_use callback exists in Anomaly. The NPC medkit handler heals directly without firing any event -- the NPC heals, and nobody knows it happened. AlifePlus hooks the handler at runtime, calls the original, and emits a synthetic callback without modifying base scripts.

> "A complex AI system with a life simulation system that enables any monster and NPC to act independently on any game level, with creatures having a full life cycle where they hunt, feed, take rest, and sleep."
> -- Oles Shishkovtsov, ModDB interview [3]

If monsters were designed to converge on corpses, they should converge on the wounded too.

### Harvest: Hunt

When an NPC or the player picks up an artefact, nearby outlaws see an opportunity and pursue the carrier. Real movement, real risk. The carrier can fight back or outrun them.

The engine fires item take callbacks for both actor and NPCs. Artefact spawning was partially implemented at the engine level and expanded in Anomaly scripts, and NPC artefact gathering exists with community filters and timers. The artefact economy was clearly planned and partially implemented, but nobody added the competitive response -- when someone picks up something valuable, others should care.

> "In early versions of the simulator, a character knew one or several dealers, which had sets of quests that they generated based on the map of anomalous activity and requests from organizations, which wanted to get rich from artifacts found in the Zone."
> -- Dmitriy Iassenev, Game Developer (2008) [1]

Artefacts had value in Iassenev's original vision. NPCs competed for them. Dealers drove the economy. AlifePlus restores the competitive response that makes artefacts worth fighting over.

### Stash: Loot, Ambush, Fill

A squad transitions at a smart terrain, discovers a nearby stash, and walks there to loot it, camp to ambush others, or hide supplies inside. The NPC physically walks the distance and can be intercepted, killed, or beaten to the stash.

The engine provides inventory boxes as containers and the item take callback for actor-only interaction. Anomaly's treasure_manager indexes all inventory boxes on load, fills half with random loot, and tracks them for the player -- but NPCs never touch stashes. All NPC involvement with stashes is narrative: surrender dialogs reveal locations, task rewards mark them on the map, and PDA messages hint at contents. The `npc_on_item_take_from_box` callback exists but its handler is empty. The infrastructure was built and nobody connected it.

Stashes sit across the Zone ignored by every NPC, waiting exclusively for the player. That is the simplified version that shipped, not the intended design.

> "The character would take on a quest to search for the artifact, go to the approximate area in which it could be found, if able to find the artifact, brought it back to the dealer, traded it, then picked a new quest."
> -- Dmitriy Iassenev, Game Developer (2008) [1]

Iassenev described this exact loop working internally at GSC and regretted it never shipped. If NPCs can find artefacts and trade them, they can find stashes and interact with their contents. AlifePlus connects the infrastructure that was left hanging.

### Area: Conquer

When a squad discovers an unguarded smart terrain nearby, it walks there and claims the area if it arrives alive.

The engine's smart zone is a bare scheduling shell with zero faction fields -- all territory control is Lua-side. Anomaly derives faction from which stalkers are physically present, and `sim_board` gates squad-to-smart assignment by faction and time of day. The faction system exists and the time-gating exists, but squads never proactively decide to claim territory.

AlifePlus extends this with radiant conquest: when a squad conquers a smart, the arrival handler sets faction and respawn parameters at runtime so the engine spawns the conqueror's faction. Conquered smarts are tracked with FIFO eviction and persist across save/load. Territory changes hands through movement, not through scripts.

> "A control point leading to a place with artifacts is controlled by bandits. Their destruction means that the path to resources is safe."
> -- Clear Sky pre-release interview [5]

> "The more points with artifacts the faction controls, the cooler stalkers it can recruit."
> -- Clear Sky pre-release interview [5]

### Needs: 9 Drives, 15 Consequences

When a squad transitions at a smart terrain, it evaluates nine human needs and pursues whichever is strongest.

Drive scoring uses Hull's Drive Reduction Theory (1943) weighted by Maslow's hierarchy of needs (1943). Drive = weight * (elapsed / threshold)^2. Weights follow Maslow -- survival outweighs security outweighs belonging. The squared deprivation ratio adapts Hull: drive grows nonlinearly with deprivation, so mild discomfort is noise and critical need dominates. The combination produces a single prioritized decision from competing needs -- exactly the "algorithm for choice of current needs" that Iassenev described wanting to build.

This is not a novel invention. Hull and Maslow were the dominant behavioral models when GSC designed A-Life in 2002-2004. The engine's need states (eat, rest, sleep, hunt) map directly to Maslow's physiological tier. `GetSatiety()` is a continuous deprivation variable -- the same concept Hull formalized as drive strength. GSC was building drive theory into the engine. They ran out of time.

The nine drives:

- **Hunger** -- finds a campfire, eats food from inventory (bread, sausage, canned goods)
- **Sleep** -- waits for nightfall, finds a campfire, sleeps through the night
- **Rest** -- finds a campfire, smokes a cigarette, has a drink
- **Heal** -- returns to a friendly base, uses a medkit, bandage, or stimpack
- **Shelter** -- returns to a friendly base when exposed too long
- **Money** -- searches anomaly fields for artefacts, or hunts mutant lairs
- **Supply** -- visits a trader, trades an artefact for AP ammo, grenades, or medical supplies
- **Job** -- guards their base, explores the Zone, researches anomalies. Monolith and Greh worship. Military and Duty drill
- **Social** -- finds a campfire or returns to base, shares cigarettes and drinks

NPCs consume real items from their inventory on arrival. A guard smokes a cigarette on duty. A hungry stalker eats food he was carrying. A supply run costs an artefact and returns ammunition. If the NPC has nothing to consume, the need is still addressed -- he walked there and the drive resets.

The engine source contains the scaffolding:

- `GetSatiety()` returns hardcoded 0.5, `ChangeSatiety()` does nothing -- hunger was planned and abandoned
- `eStateRest` with Sleep, `eStateEat` with the full corpse lifecycle -- rest and eat states exist but nothing triggers them by need
- `sr_camp.script` runs campfire social dynamics -- social behavior exists at smarts
- `gulag_general.script` assigns campfire, rest, and guard jobs -- the job system exists
- `xr_eat_medkit.script` handles NPC healing -- health response exists

The infrastructure exists. Nobody connected it.

> "It was planned that character behaviour would diversify due to introduction of requirements of food and sleep."
> -- Dmitriy Iassenev, Game Developer (2008) [1]

> "Now, instead of creation of an algorithm for choice of current needs and their satisfaction, a simpler model has been used, which offloaded this work to the smart terrains."
> -- Dmitriy Iassenev, Game Developer (2008) [1]

> "I would very much like to play a game in which characters would live their own lives, each would have his own goal in the game, each would have human-like (or some specific for monsters) needs that the character would have to satisfy."
> -- Dmitriy Iassenev, Game Developer (2008) [1]

> "A complex AI system with a life simulation system that enables any monster and NPC to act independently on any game level, with creatures having a full life cycle where they hunt, feed, take rest, and sleep."
> -- Oles Shishkovtsov, ModDB interview [3]

AlifePlus builds the algorithm Iassenev wanted -- the "algorithm for choice of current needs and their satisfaction" that was replaced with "a simpler model" before shipping. Hull scoring selects the strongest need. Maslow weighting ensures survival dominates when it matters. The engine's need states finally trigger by need instead of by smart terrain assignment.

**Why NPCs consume items.** `GetSatiety()` returning 0.5 proves GSC planned a hunger system. `eStateEat` proves they planned eating behavior. Iassenev explicitly said food and sleep needs were planned. An NPC that walks to a campfire because he is hungry and then eats nothing is the fake version. AlifePlus makes the need real by consuming what the NPC is actually carrying.

**Why supply trading costs an artefact.** Iassenev described the artefact economy loop: find artefact, bring to dealer, trade, pick new quest. The supply consequence implements exactly this loop. A supply run that gives free items is not a trade -- it is a spawn. Trading an artefact for ammunition is the economy GSC designed.
